# Sample Codebook for Open-Ended Responses

This codebook was used to support descriptive, participant-level thematic coding of open-ended survey responses from final-year undergraduate computer science students. Each participant provided three open-ended answers about prior single-sex schooling, confidence-related hesitation in university, and perceived advantages or disadvantages of coeducational university education for future career and social life.

Coding was conducted at the participant level. The three open-ended responses from each participant were treated as one response set. Themes were non-mutually exclusive, meaning that more than one theme could be assigned to the same participant. A theme was coded as present if it appeared at least once across the participant’s response set. Repeated mentions of the same theme by the same participant were counted only once.

The codebook was developed with LLM-assisted support and human oversight. The LLM was used only to suggest candidate themes, labels, and wording refinements. Final theme definitions, merging or removal of themes, and all coding decisions were made by the researcher. Raw open-ended responses are not shared publicly to reduce re-identification risk.

## Coding Format

Each theme was coded as a binary variable:

- `1` = the theme is present in the participant’s response set.
- `0` = the theme is not present in the participant’s response set.

## Theme Definitions

| Code | Theme | Operational Definition | Include When | Exclude When | Paraphrased Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| T1 | Women-only comfort | The participant describes feeling more comfortable, confident, relaxed, or able to participate in a women-only educational environment. | The response links single-sex or women-only education to comfort, confidence, easier communication, or freer participation. | The response only mentions academic quality or grades without referring to comfort or confidence. | “Being in an all-female environment made it easier for me to speak and participate without feeling uncomfortable.” |
| T2 | Initial coeducational shock | The participant describes the transition to a coeducational university as initially difficult, stressful, unfamiliar, or emotionally challenging. | The response mentions early difficulty adapting to mixed-gender classrooms or academic interaction after single-sex schooling. | The response discusses mixed-gender interaction only as a general issue, without describing an initial transition or shock. | “At first, entering a mixed university classroom felt unfamiliar and stressful because I had not experienced that environment before.” |
| T3 | Classroom silence | The participant describes remaining silent, avoiding speaking, not asking questions, or not participating in class despite having something to say. | The response mentions silence, hesitation to ask questions, avoiding discussion, or not presenting ideas in class. | The response only mentions low confidence in general without describing silence or reduced participation. | “Even when I knew the answer or had a question, I preferred to stay quiet in class.” |
| T4 | Fear of judgment | The participant describes fear of being judged, evaluated negatively, laughed at, misunderstood, or seen as less capable. | The response links non-participation or hesitation to fear of others’ opinions, mistakes, embarrassment, or negative evaluation. | The response describes shyness or silence without mentioning judgment, embarrassment, or others’ reactions. | “I avoided asking questions because I was worried others would think my question was simple or that I was not competent.” |
| T5 | Male-peer communication gap | The participant describes difficulty, distance, discomfort, or lack of practice in communicating with male classmates or male colleagues. | The response refers to limited interaction with male peers, uncertainty in mixed-gender communication, or difficulty working with men. | The response discusses workplace anxiety without specifically referring to communication with male peers. | “Because I had little academic interaction with male classmates before, communicating with them in university felt difficult.” |
| T6 | Gradual adaptation | The participant describes becoming more comfortable over time, learning to communicate, or adapting to the mixed-gender university environment. | The response includes change over time, improvement, increased confidence, or learning how to interact in coeducational settings. | The response only states that coeducation is useful without describing adaptation or personal change. | “Although it was difficult at first, I gradually became more comfortable speaking and working with classmates.” |
| T7 | Workplace preparation | The participant describes coeducational university as useful preparation for future work, professional communication, teamwork, or social life. | The response connects mixed-gender university experience to readiness for workplaces, internships, professional teams, or future social interaction. | The response only mentions that coeducation is normal or common without linking it to preparation. | “Studying in a mixed university helped me practice communication that will be necessary in future work environments.” |
| T8 | Mixed-workplace anxiety | The participant expresses concern, stress, or lack of readiness about entering a future mixed-gender workplace. | The response mentions worry about professional communication, workplace interaction, or future mixed-gender work environments. | The response discusses general job-market anxiety unrelated to gender composition or communication. | “I am worried that working in a mixed-gender professional environment will be difficult because I have limited experience with it.” |
| T9 | Limited social learning | The participant describes single-sex education as limiting social skills, reducing exposure to mixed-gender interaction, or restricting social development. | The response links single-sex education to limited social experience, lack of mixed-gender practice, or weaker social preparation. | The response only mentions academic focus or comfort in single-sex education without describing limitation. | “The women-only environment was comfortable, but it did not give enough opportunity to learn mixed-gender communication.” |
| T10 | Academic focus | The participant describes the single-sex or women-only environment as helping concentration, reducing distraction, or supporting academic seriousness. | The response links the gender environment to better focus, fewer distractions, or stronger academic attention. | The response mentions confidence or comfort without referring to focus or academic concentration. | “The single-sex environment helped me focus more on studying and academic progress.” |
| T11 | Perceived gender bias | The participant describes gender-based unfairness, stereotypes, unequal treatment, or concern that women’s technical ability may be undervalued. | The response refers to being judged because of gender, unequal expectations, male dominance, or women’s expertise being overlooked. | The response mentions general fear of judgment without connecting it to gender. | “I sometimes felt that women had to prove their technical ability more than men.” |

## Notes on Interpretation

These themes are descriptive and should be interpreted as indicators of how participants described their experiences, not as causal evidence. Because the coding was based on self-reported open-ended responses and conducted by a single human coder with LLM-assisted support, the thematic results are used to contextualize the quantitative findings rather than to provide independently replicated qualitative evidence.

Theme prevalence was calculated as the percentage of participants in each female group who mentioned each theme at least once. Because themes were non-mutually exclusive, percentages do not sum to 100%.
